📖 Tent Buying Guide: 5 Key Decisions

1. Capacity: Can 2 People Sleep in a 2-Person Tent?

Short answer: yes — like sardines. Tent ratings assume shoulder-to-shoulder with zero gear space. In reality:

  • Solo camping — buy a 2P tent. Extra space for your pack and boots.
  • Two people — buy a 3P tent. Room for two plus gear.
  • Family camping — subtract 2 from the rating. Family of 4 needs a 6P tent.
  • Ultralight solo — a 1P tent works. Every ounce counts.

2. 3-Season vs 4-Season: When Is Double the Price Worth It?

3-Season tents cover spring through fall. They are lighter, cheaper, and better ventilated. A good one handles surprise storms and light snow. 4-Season tents handle 50+ mph winds and heavy snow loads, but they are heavy, expensive ($600+), and stifling in summer. You only need one if: sustained winds >40 mph, snow accumulation >6 inches, or winter mountaineering.

Bottom line: A 3-season tent serves 95% of campers. Do not buy a 4-season tent thinking it is just a "tougher" 3-season. In summer, it is a sweatbox.

3. Weight: Backpacking vs Car Camping

Backpacking: Weight is everything. Keep your tent under 3 lbs. If budget allows, go under 2.5 lbs. Car camping: Weight does not matter. Buy the roomiest tent. You are walking 50 feet from the trunk.

4. Waterproofing: Understanding Hydrostatic Head

Tent waterproofness is measured in millimeters of hydrostatic head. Higher = more waterproof:

  • Below 1500mm — light rain OK, downpour means leaks. Budget tents live here.
  • 1500-3000mm — handles most rain. Mid-range standard.
  • 3000mm+ — torrential rain is no problem. MSR, Big Agnes, Hilleberg class.
  • Bathtub floor — edges curve up so water cannot flow in. More important than the rating.

5. Freestanding vs Non-Freestanding

Freestanding — stands on its own with poles. Can be moved after setup. 90% of campers choose this. Non-freestanding — must be staked to stand. Lighter but trickier. Common in ultralight trekking pole tents.

❓ Tent FAQ

Q: Is the most expensive tent always the best?

No. $500+ tents excel at saving weight, not at keeping rain out. A $130 Coleman does that just fine. If you are car camping, that extra $370 buys a lot of steak.

Q: How long does a tent last?

5-10 years with normal use. The first thing to fail is seam tape (3-5 years). Fix it with Seam Grip. Premium tents last longer with better poles and materials.

Q: Can I use a 3-season tent all year?

Yes — if there are no extreme storms. But pair it with a warmer sleeping bag and pad for winter. If winds exceed 40 mph, you need a 4-season tent — 3-season poles can bend or snap.

Q: Do I really need a footprint?

Strongly recommended. Prevents punctures, keeps the floor dry, and extends tent life. Buy the official one or cut your own from Tyvek — same function, $10 vs $50.

Q: Single-wall vs double-wall tent?

Double-wall (recommended) — separate rainfly with an air gap. Condensation forms on the fly, not on you. Single-wall is lighter but you will wake up with condensation on your face.
