📖 Sleeping Bag Buying Guide: Down vs Synthetic Explained

Down — Light, Durable, Expensive

Down is the gold standard for outdoor sleeping bags. Its key advantage is warmth-to-weight ratio — for the same warmth, down is 30-50% lighter than synthetic. It also packs much smaller. A 20°F down bag compresses to basketball size.

Fill Power (FP) is everything:

  • 550-600 FP (Kelty Cosmic) — Entry-level. Decent warmth, budget-friendly.
  • 700-800 FP (Marmot Phase) — Mid-to-high end. The backpacker sweet spot.
  • 850-950 FP (WM UltraLite, FF Hummingbird) — Top tier. Every ounce generates max warmth.

The fatal flaw: Down loses ALL insulating power when wet. In humid environments (Pacific Northwest, Appalachian Trail), protect it carefully or choose synthetic.

Synthetic — Affordable, Wet-Proof, Heavier

Synthetic insulation has one killer advantage: it keeps you warm even when wet. Even if your bag gets soaked by rain or condensation, synthetic still insulates — down cannot. It is also cheaper, easier to wash, and tougher.

Downsides: 30-50% heavier than down. Larger packed size. Insulation degrades noticeably after 5-8 years of heavy use.

🌡️ Understanding Temperature Ratings

The number on the bag (e.g. 20°F) is the EN/ISO lower limit, NOT the comfort temperature:

  • Comfort rating — An average woman sleeps comfortably. Usually 10-15°F warmer than the bag listing.
  • Lower limit — An average man can sleep 8 hours without waking. This is what most bags advertise.
  • Extreme rating — Survival only. You will shiver but might not freeze to death in 6 hours.

The golden rule: Expected nighttime low + 15°F = the bag rating you should buy. Camp expecting 35°F? Buy a 20°F bag.

❓ Sleeping Bag FAQ

Q: Mummy vs rectangular bag?

Mummy — Tapered fit hugs your body. Warmest, lightest. 90% of backpackers use these. Rectangular — Roomy and familiar like your bed at home. Heavier, colder. Best for car camping or people who hate feeling constricted.

Q: How do I wash a sleeping bag?

1-2 times per year is plenty. Use down-specific detergent (Nikwax Down Wash). Never use regular laundry detergent — it strips the natural oils from down. Dry on low heat with 2-3 tennis balls to break up clumps.

Q: How long does a sleeping bag last?

A well-cared-for down bag: 10-20 years. Synthetic: 5-8 years before insulation degrades. The most critical habit: never store your bag compressed. Hang it or use a large breathable storage sack.
